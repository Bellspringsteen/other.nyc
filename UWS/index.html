01 · Method

Water as a population proxy

Every building over 25,000 square feet in New York City must annually report its energy and water consumption to the city under Local Law 84. That data is public. We downloaded it.

The core idea is simple: people use water. If a building has 100 apartments and shows water usage consistent with 5 people, something is off. By comparing actual water use against a theoretical full-occupancy baseline, we can estimate how full a building really is — regardless of what the census says, regardless of what the landlord claims.

The Calculation

NYC DEP baseline: ~50 gallons per person per day (residential average)

Full occupancy benchmark: 2.3 people/unit × 50 gal/day × 365 days = 42,000 gal/unit/year

Implied occupancy = actual water use ÷ full-occupancy benchmark

02 · The Finding

Large UWS buildings appear full

The short version: the "empty Upper West Side" narrative doesn't hold for the large apartment stock. By water metrics, the big multifamily buildings are running at or above theoretical full occupancy. The median UWS large building shows water consumption consistent with full occupancy.

This is a different story than you might expect. High rents haven't emptied the large apartment towers. The data suggests these buildings — pre-war walk-ups turned luxury, mid-century coops, newer rentals — are occupied. People are there.

"The water tells us that most of the Upper West Side's large apartment buildings are occupied. The story of urban vacancy is more complicated than headline numbers suggest."

There is, however, real variation. Some buildings show dramatically low water use. And the dataset can't speak to what it doesn't cover: smaller buildings, luxury condos sold as pied-à-terres, or units held vacant while on the market.

03 · Distribution

Some buildings are nearly empty

Zoom in on the distribution of individual UWS buildings and a different picture emerges. While most buildings cluster near full occupancy, a meaningful tail shows buildings with surprisingly low water use — consistent with significant vacancy.

UWS Buildings by Implied Occupancy Bucket (% of units in each range)

About 5.6% of buildings — accounting for 5.6% of covered units — show implied occupancy below 50% of capacity. A further ~16% are in the 50–75% "moderate" range. These outliers may represent:

  • Buildings undergoing renovation or between tenancies
  • Institutional or mixed-use properties with non-residential water
  • Reporting errors in the LL84 submission
  • Genuine structural vacancies — buildings that have shed population

04 · Neighborhood Comparison

How the UWS compares

The UWS isn't alone. We ran the same analysis across seven NYC neighborhoods, from Tribeca to Washington Heights. The results reveal real geographic variation in how water — and by proxy, people — flows through the city's housing stock.

Implied Occupancy by Neighborhood — % of Full-Occupancy Baseline

A few notable patterns:

  • Washington Heights shows the highest implied occupancy, consistent with its status as a dense working-class neighborhood with larger household sizes.
  • Park Slope shows the lowest — 93% — perhaps reflecting more empty-nesters, smaller households, or better water efficiency in newer buildings.
  • The UWS sits near the middle at 105%, indistinguishable from comparable neighborhoods by this metric alone.
  • Tribeca and Chelsea, often cited as high-vacancy luxury neighborhoods, actually show high water use — these LL84 buildings are primarily rental apartment towers, not the luxury condos that generate pied-à-terre concerns.
Methodological note: Washington Heights shows unusually high implied occupancy (169%). This likely reflects larger average household sizes (the neighborhood has a large Dominican-American community), older plumbing with higher leak rates, and the possibility that the LL84 sample skews toward the largest, most amenitized buildings in each area — which may not be representative of the full housing stock.

05 · Building Era

Do older buildings use more water?

Older buildings tend to have less efficient plumbing — more leaks, older fixtures, no WaterSense retrofits. If pre-war buildings systematically show higher water use than modern ones, it could inflate our occupancy estimates for neighborhoods with older housing stock.

UWS Median Implied Occupancy by Building Era

The data does show pre-war buildings running slightly higher than modern ones, though the difference is modest. This supports the idea that our 50 gal/person/day baseline may be slightly optimistic for the UWS's predominantly pre-war stock — meaning the true occupancy rate might be somewhat lower than the headline number suggests. 06 · What the Data Can't Say

The invisible housing stock

This analysis covers large multifamily buildings — the kind with more than 25,000 square feet of floor area. That captures the classic UWS pre-war apartment towers, the big mid-century co-ops, the newer doorman buildings.

The pied-à-terre phenomenon — wealthy buyers who use NYC apartments as occasional crash pads rather than primary residences — is real and documented. But it tends to concentrate in high-end condominiums, not the large rental buildings and co-ops that dominate the LL84 dataset. A full picture would require combining this water analysis with condo sales records and voter registration data.

"The UWS's large apartment buildings are occupied. The vacancy story, if it exists, lives elsewhere: in the luxury condos, the brownstone floors, the units held off-market. That story requires different data."

The 2021 NYC Housing Vacancy Survey found citywide residential vacancy at 4.5%, with much higher rates in Manhattan luxury buildings. But "vacancy" as measured by census surveys counts units that are legally available but unoccupied — not units owned by non-residents who use them part-time. The true "functional vacancy" rate is likely higher, and water data is one of the few ways to approach it.

07 · Methodology & Caveats

How we did this

Data Sources

LL84 Benchmarking (2022) — NYC Open Data. Annual water and energy reports for all buildings ≥25,000 sq ft. We filtered to residential use types (Multifamily Housing, Residence Hall/Dormitory, Senior Living Community).

PLUTO (MapPLUTO 2023) — NYC Open Data. Used for building age and class enrichment via BBL join.

Census ACS 2022 — Population and housing unit estimates are synthetic approximations based on published ACS 5-year estimates and the 2021 NYC Housing Vacancy Survey. A Census API key is required for live data pulls; see the source code for the query.

Known Limitations

  • Non-residential water use: Multifamily buildings include laundry rooms, gyms, pools, cooling towers, and commercial tenants. These inflate the per-unit water figure and push implied occupancy above 100%.
  • Plumbing efficiency: Pre-war buildings have higher leak rates and older fixtures. Our 50 gal/person/day baseline may be too conservative for older stock.
  • Self-reported data: LL84 figures come from building owners; some are estimated rather than metered. Estimated readings are flagged in the dataset but not excluded here.
  • Coverage bias: The 25,000 sq ft threshold means only large buildings are covered. Smaller buildings may behave differently.
  • Outlier exclusion: Buildings reporting more than 150 kgal/unit/year were excluded as likely data errors or buildings with major non-residential water use (65 buildings removed).
