01 · Method

Water as a population proxy

Every building over 25,000 square feet in New York City must annually report its energy and water consumption to the city under Local Law 84. That data is public. We downloaded it.

The core idea is simple: people use water. By comparing actual building-level water use against the expected consumption if each unit were occupied at a given household size, we can estimate how many people are actually living in each building — regardless of what the census says, regardless of what the landlord claims.

The Calculation

NYC DEP baseline: ~50 gallons per person per day (residential average)

Implied residents = annual water use ÷ (50 gal/day × 365 days)

Occupancy rate = implied residents per unit ÷ 2.3 (NYC avg household size)

At 50 gal/day, the UWS aggregate implied occupancy is 105.3% of baseline (2.3 ppl/unit). Washington Heights: 169.5%. The gap between them is robust across all assumptions.

Important: This dataset (LL84) only covers buildings ≥25,000 sq ft — primarily large rental apartment towers and co-ops. It covers ~73% of estimated UWS housing units. Smaller buildings, townhouses, and most condos are not included.
02 · The Wrong Baseline

Why "105% occupancy" is the wrong headline

Our original analysis led with a reassuring number: the UWS's large buildings show water usage consistent with 105% of theoretical full occupancy. The buildings are full. Case closed.

But that framing contains a buried assumption: that "full occupancy" means 2.3 people per unit — the current NYC average household size. That average already reflects decades of demographic change: rising rents pricing out families, rent-stabilized apartments passing to single occupants, adult children who never moved back. The baseline itself is the story.

"The buildings aren't empty. But the question isn't whether anyone is home — it's whether the right number of people are home given how the building was built."

The UWS's pre-war apartment stock was built in the 1910s–1930s for households of four, five, six people. Three-bedroom and four-bedroom apartments were standard, not luxury. Measuring those apartments against a 2.3-person baseline is like measuring a concert hall by whether it has any audience at all, rather than whether it's anywhere near sold out.

04 · Inside the Family Apartments

What the large UWS units actually contain

Across the 17,401 large pre-war UWS units (over 1,400 sq ft, built before 1960), we can estimate the household type based on implied water consumption. The picture is of a neighborhood that has lost its families without losing its apartments.

Nearly half the family-sized apartment stock on the Upper West Side is occupied by couples or singles. Only 14% show water use consistent with a real family — three or more adults, or parents and children sharing the space the building was designed for.

That 29,000 figure covers only the largest pre-war buildings in our dataset. Extending the analysis to all UWS units by their size tier suggests a total gap — relative to what the building stock could hold if occupied at family scale — of approximately 74,000 people across the full large-building dataset.

"The UWS isn't emptied by vacancy. It's emptied by underpopulation. The apartments are full. The bedrooms are not."

This is a structural shift, not a seasonal one. It shows up consistently across building vintages, across zip codes within the UWS, and across building sizes. Washington Heights — with the same building type, built the same era — demonstrates that these apartments can and do house families when economic conditions support it.

What this means for the city

The UWS has approximately 88.5 million square feet of residential floor area in buildings large enough to report to LL84. At Washington Heights density (3.85 people per 1,000 sqft vs. 1.91), that same building stock would house roughly 170,000 additional people — without a single new unit built.

The housing crisis on the Upper West Side is not primarily a shortage of apartments. It's a mismatch between who occupies the apartments and what those apartments were built for.

06 · Building-Level Distribution

Most buildings cluster near baseline — the outliers matter

Measured against the conventional 2.3-person baseline, most UWS buildings land near or above 100%. But that distribution itself tells the story: a neighborhood-scale bell curve centered on "couple occupancy," not family occupancy.

08 · Building Era

Pre-war buildings: the family stock

The UWS's pre-war building stock (built before 1940) accounts for 65% of all units in our dataset. These are the large-unit family-scale buildings. Their median implied occupancy of 103% — just above the 2.3 baseline — understates the story: these buildings were designed for 4–5 people per unit, not 2.3.

UWS Median Implied Occupancy by Building Era (baseline = 2.3 ppl/unit)

Modern buildings (2010+) show lower implied occupancy by water — consistent with more efficient fixtures and smaller average units. Pre-war buildings run slightly higher, partly from older plumbing and leaks, partly from larger units that can house more people when families do live in them. 09 · What the Data Can't Say

The limits of this analysis

This analysis covers large multifamily buildings — those over 25,000 square feet — primarily the classic UWS pre-war apartment towers and big mid-century co-ops. It does not cover:

~27%
Of UWS housing units in smaller buildings — not captured by LL84
Many
Luxury condos sold to non-resident investors ("pied-à-terre" units)
8.5%
Census-estimated vacancy rate (synthetic ACS estimate)

The water signal cannot distinguish between a 3-bedroom apartment with one person and one where a family of three lives. It only tells us how much water the building used. The bedroom-count estimates in this analysis are inferences from square footage, not from floor plan data.

What the comparison with Washington Heights does establish is that the gap is real and large: buildings of similar vintage, similar size, different neighborhood economics produce radically different population density. That gap shows up in the water, consistently, across hundreds of buildings.

A complete picture of UWS underpopulation would combine this water analysis with NYC DOF condo sales records, voter registration addresses, and school enrollment data. Each tells a piece of the same story.

10 · Methodology & Caveats

How we did this

Data Sources

LL84 Benchmarking (2022) — NYC Open Data. Annual water and energy reports for all buildings ≥25,000 sq ft. Filtered to residential use types (Multifamily Housing, Residence Hall/Dormitory, Senior Living Community).

PLUTO (MapPLUTO 2023) — NYC Open Data. Used for building age and class enrichment via BBL join.

Family capacity estimates — derived from LL84 Gross Floor Area divided by reported unit counts, using a ~500 sqft/bedroom rule of thumb for NYC pre-war stock. All bedroom and capacity estimates are approximations.

Known Limitations

  • Non-residential water use: Multifamily buildings include laundry rooms, gyms, pools, and cooling towers. These inflate implied occupancy — Washington Heights' higher figure may partly reflect older plumbing and higher leak rates, not only larger households.
  • GFA vs. net residential area: LL84 reports Gross Floor Area, which includes hallways, mechanical rooms, and common space (~20–25% of total). Bedroom estimates are conservative accordingly.
  • Self-reported data: LL84 figures come from building owners; some are estimated rather than metered.
  • Outlier exclusion: Buildings reporting more than 150 kgal/unit/year were excluded as likely data errors or buildings with major non-residential water use.
